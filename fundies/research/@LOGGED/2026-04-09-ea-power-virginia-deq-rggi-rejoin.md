# Energy Aspects — Virginia DEQ Confirms RGGI Rejoin 1 July (2026-04-09)

## Summary

On 9 April the Virginia DEQ posted regulations confirming the state will rejoin RGGI on 1 July for a six-month compliance period covering H2 2026. Energy Aspects has raised their peak-summer West Hub spark forecast by $3.69/MWh to $52.87/MWh. This reverses EA's prior view (2 April) that a July rejoin was unlikely.

## Key Points

- Virginia DEQ posted final regulations on 9 April — state rejoins RGGI on **1 July 2026**
- Six-month compliance period for H2 2026
- H2 26 emissions budget: **11.5 Mst**, short of estimated **15 Mst** Virginia emissions in H2 26
- RGGI still expected to be in surplus this year, but by **~6 Mst** (vs ~10 Mst without Virginia)
- EA raised peak-summer West Hub spark forecast by **$3.69/MWh** to **$52.87/MWh**
- Regulations reinstate Virginia using emissions budgets from the state's original RGGI participation
- Negotiations with 10 RGGI states on revised emissions supply for 2027+ (Third Program Review alignment) are ongoing
- DEQ has until year-end to complete negotiations and promulgate revised regulations for 2027+

## Trading Implications

- **Summer sparks**: Bullish — $3.69/MWh uplift to WH peak-summer spark now baked in; prior forecast ($49.18) was already at decadal highs
- **RGGI allowance demand**: Virginia's 3.5 Mst shortfall (15 Mst emissions vs 11.5 Mst budget) will tighten the RGGI surplus from ~10 Mst to ~6 Mst — watch for allowance price reaction
- **DOM congestion**: Reinforces structural bullish thesis — higher VA fossil dispatch costs increase import reliance and east-flowing constraint risk
- **AD-West spread**: Could marginally widen in peak seasons due to countervailing energy and congestion effects — system energy prices rise when DOM plants are on the margin, but more western capacity (often AEP) gets called to flow over west/east interfaces into DOM and PEPCO, raising utilization on transfer interfaces and increasing congestion in DOM/PEPCO (and to a lesser degree WH) while congesting AD down. In milder periods when interfaces are unconstrained, no spread impact — RGGI dispatch changes are reflected in the system-wide energy price only
- **2027+ uncertainty**: Third Program Review negotiations could further tighten emissions budgets; DEQ has until end of 2026 to finalize

## EA Emissions Team Follow-Up (2026-04-14)

Email from Brian (EA emissions team) responding to Edi's question on WH and AD-West spread impact:

> Our emissions team published an alert today officially changing our base case to July 1 for rejoining, and we are consequently raising our WH NQ 26 spark forecast by $3.69/MWh. In terms of impact on AD-West, we don't have a number per se, but in general it could marginally widen that spread in peak seasons because of countervailing energy and congestion impacts. System energy prices will rise when DOM plants are on the margin, but this will also call on more western capacity in PJM, often in AEP, to flow power over the west/east interfaces and into DOM and PEPCO. That will also raise utilization on those transfer interfaces, which raises the congestion component of LMPs in DOM, PEPCO, and to a lesser degree at West Hub while often congesting AD down. In milder periods when those interfaces are not constrained, there should be no impact on the spread as any change in dispatch due to RGGI will be reflected in the system-wide energy price.
