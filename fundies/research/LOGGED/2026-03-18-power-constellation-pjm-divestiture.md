# Constellation Sells ~4.4 GW PJM Gas Assets to LS Power for $5B

## Summary

On March 18, Constellation announced the sale of approximately 4.4 GW of natural gas-fired generation in PJM to LS Power for $5 billion (~$1,142/kW). The assets — Bethlehem, Hay Road, Edge Moor (Delaware), and York 1 & 2 (Pennsylvania) — are all thermal, primarily gas-fired plants in the Mid-Atlantic. The sale satisfies the largest portion of divestitures mandated by the DOJ and FERC as a condition of Constellation's Calpine acquisition, which closed January 7, 2026 and made Constellation the world's largest private power generator. The deal is expected to close later in 2026, subject to regulatory approvals.

## Key Points

- **Assets**: ~4.4 GW across five plants — Bethlehem, Hay Road, Edge Moor (DE), York 1 & 2 (PA)
- **Fuel**: All thermal, primarily natural gas-fired
- **Price**: $5 billion (~**$1,142/kW**)
- **Buyer**: LS Power Equity Advisors
- **Rationale**: DOJ/FERC-mandated divestiture to preserve market competitiveness post-Calpine acquisition
- **Timeline**: Expected to close later in 2026
- **Valuation comparison**: Vistra's purchase of Cogentrix assets (5.5 GW across PJM, ISO-NE, ERCOT) came in at ~$4B (~**$730/kW**) — Constellation/LS Power deal commands a **56% premium** on a $/kW basis

## Trading Implications

- **No net PJM capacity change** — assets stay in PJM under LS Power ownership; no supply impact
- **$1,142/kW sets a new benchmark** for PJM gas asset valuations — well above Vistra/Cogentrix at $730/kW; reflects the market pricing in structural demand growth and tightening reserve margins
- **LS Power's bet on PJM demand** — CEO Paul Segal: "PJM is at the epicenter of the surge in electricity demand" — validates the datacenter-driven load growth thesis across your other readings
- **Mid-Atlantic concentration** — all assets in DE/PA; no direct AEP/Dominion zone exposure, but the premium signals conviction that PJM-wide tightening lifts all zones
- **Forced sale context** — Constellation had to divest, so LS Power had negotiating leverage; the fact that it still priced at $1,142/kW despite being a forced seller is notable
